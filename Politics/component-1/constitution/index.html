    Key Concepts:

    Codified - A Constitution in which all provisions are written down in a single set of documents, for example the US constitution.

    Uncodified - A Constitution that is not contained in a single set of documents, for example the British Constitution

    What is a Constitution?

    A constitution is a set of fundamental principles or established precedents that define the structure, powers, and duties of a government. It outlines the rights and responsibilities of citizens and the rules for how the state operates. Constitutions can be written (Codified) or unwritten (Uncodified), and they serve as the highest legal authority in a country.

    What are the Sources of the British Constitution?

    Common Law

    The rights and laws passed down over the years by legal judgements in courts

    Otherwise known as "Judicial Precedence"

    Conventions

    Conventions aren't written down, but are generally agreed rules and procedures

    An example is the Royal Assent, where the bills must be passed through the monarch

    Magna Carta

    A Royal charter of rights between King John and his barons in response to political crisis

    There was 63 clauses, now only 4 are relevant

    Has influenced many documents, such as the European Convention on Human Rights

    Statute Law

    Acts of Parliament

    Before the UK left the EU in Jan 2020, EU laws such as the Lisbon Treaty took precedence over laws passed by Westminster and were binding on the UK government. This represented a clear limit on parliamentary sovereignty

    Royal Prerogative

    The residual or remaining powers exercised in the name of the crown.

    In modern times, The Royal Prerogative could be seen as a key factor in boosting the powers of the Prime Minister

    Works of Authority

    Arguably the least visible/clear source of the British Constitution

    Books and documents that deal with areas including parliamentary procedures and government duties

    Recent changes to the constitution

    Brexit

    2017-2020; The UK's withdrawl from the EU

    Extension of the powers of the Supreme Court

    During the Brexit process, the UK Supreme Court's powers were extended, particularly around limiting the prerogative powers of the Prime Minister. This includes the controversial case in 2019 where the Court ruled that Prime Minister Boris Johnson's prorogation (suspending) of Parliament was unlawful. This was seen as a way of limiting the executive's ability to bypass Parliament during the Brexit debates.

    The Supreme Court in itself is smaller, more diverse and much less conservative dominated

    Regional Mayors

    There is now greater power and autonomy in larger cities

    This has been criticised for being too much power in the unelected

    Regional Councils

    1986, Margaret Thatcher abolished regional councils, such as Greater Manchester Council, or Merseyside Council. This was replaced with smaller ones such as Manchester City Council and other local governance. This was because Labour had a large amount of control over those councils, which Thatcher viewed as a threat

    Power of the Speaker

    The Speaker of Commons, John Bercow (now Linsday Hoyle) did not like the way that Brexit was handled, and ensured the Government was properly scrutinised

    Reforms under the Cameron Coalition

    Elected Mayors

    London was granted an elected mayor in 1998

    Under the Cameron Coalition, this was expanded to all cities, to provide local governance, and direct accountability

    Fixed Term Parliament Act (2011)

    Introduced 5 year intervals between elections

    This stops prime ministers from calling early elections at will

    Further Devolution in Scotland

    Expanded powers for the Scottish Parliament, increasing Scotland's autonomy over areas such as taxation and welfare.

    MP Recall

    Enabled constituents to initiate a recall petition to remove an MP if they have been convicted of an offense or suspended from the House of Commons for a specified period.

    This aimed to increase accountability to the constituents
